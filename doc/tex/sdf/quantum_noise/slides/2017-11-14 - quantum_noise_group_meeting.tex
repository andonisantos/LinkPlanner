\mysection{Quantum Noise}\large
\vspace{0cm}

\noindent
Quantum noise is an intrinsic property of light, it is a manifestation of the fluctuations of the quantum vacuum field.\\
\\
%In fact, there is a minimum energy level ??? (ver mark fox) of uncertanty given by the Heisenberg uncertanty
%\begin{equation}

The objective of this work is to model quantum noise in a double homodyne detection system and validate the numerical model with  experimental data.\\
\\

%--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
%------------ SLIDE-------
\mysection{Quantum Noise - Theoretical Introduction}\large
\vspace{0cm}

A Coherent states can be defined in the number state basis $\{\ket{n}\}$ as
$$
\ket{\alpha} = e^{-\frac{|\alpha|^2}{2}} \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{\alpha^n}{\sqrt{n!}} \ket{n}
$$
%in which the complex number $\alpha$ is the sole parameter that characterizes it.\\
in which the complex number $\alpha = |\alpha|^{i \theta_\alpha}$ is the sole parameter that defines it. The parameter $|\alpha|$ is equal to square root of the number of photons and $\theta_\alpha$ is the signal's phase difference to a reference oscillator.\\
\\
A number state $\ket{n}$ has exactly $n$ photons. The action of the creation $\hat{a}^\dagger$ and annihilation $\hat{a}$ operators are
$$
\hat{a} \ket{n} = \sqrt{n} \ket{n-1}
\qquad
\hat{a}^\dagger \ket{n} = \sqrt{n+1} \ket{n+1}
$$
$$
\hat{n} \ket{n} = n \ket{n}
$$
in which $\hat{n} = \hat{a}^\dagger \hat{a}$, is the number operator.


%--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
%------------ SLIDE-------
\mysection{Quantum Noise - Theoretical Introduction}\large
\vspace{0cm}

\noindent
The measurement of quadratures is based in the quantum operators
$\hat{X} = \frac{1}{2} \left( \hat{a}^\dagger + \hat{a} \right)$
and
$\hat{Y} = \frac{i}{2} \left( \hat{a}^\dagger - \hat{a} \right)$,
which have the expected values
$$
	\bra{\alpha} \hat{X} \ket{\alpha} = \textrm{Re}(\alpha),
	\qquad
	\bra{\alpha} \hat{Y} \ket{\alpha} = \textrm{Im}(\alpha)
$$
and a variance
$$
\textrm{Var}(\hat{X}) = \textrm{Var}(\hat{Y}) = \frac{1}{4}
$$
This result shows that for both quadratures, the variance of measurement is the same and independent of the value of $\alpha$.

%------------ SLIDE-------
\mysection{Double Homodyne Detection}\large
\vspace{0cm}

The simultaneous measurement of the $X$ and $Y$ quadratures can be made by the double balanced homodyne technique. The quantum description of the detection is described by the following diagram
\vspace{-1em}
\begin{figure}[h]
\label{fig:scheme_homodyne}
\centering
\includegraphics{../figures/scheme_double_homodyne.pdf}
%\caption{Balanced double homodyne detection.}
\end{figure}

This technique basically measures the phase difference between a input signal (S) and a local oscillator (LO). The figure shows the quantum mechanical description of this technique, based in annhihilation operators, in which $\hat{m}$ is the operator of the output difference between the photocurrents.\\


%--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
%------------ SLIDE-------
\mysection{Double Homodyne Detection}\large
\vspace{0cm}

\noindent
%The used technique is the double homodyne technique which starts by dividing S in two beams of equal power.
One of the beams is measures in-phase with the local oscillator, and the other is measured with a phase difference of $\pi/2$ relative to the local oscillator.\\
Given the input signal state $\ket{\alpha}$, the expected values of $\hat{m}_X$ and $\hat{m}_Y$ in shot noise units will be
%
%
%and the local oscillator state  $\ket{\beta}$ ($\beta = |\beta| e^{i\theta_\beta}$), the expected value for the two quadratures operators $\hat{m_X}$ and $\hat{m_Y}$ is\\
%
$$
	\braket{\hat{m}_X} = \left|\frac{\alpha}{\sqrt{2}}\right| \cos({\theta_\alpha}),
	\qquad
	\textrm{Var}(\hat{m}_X) = \frac{1}{4}
$$
$$
	\braket{\hat{m}_Y} =  \left|\frac{\alpha}{\sqrt{2}}\right| \sin({\theta_\alpha}),
	\qquad
	\textrm{Var}(\hat{m}_Y) = \frac{1}{4}
$$
